Broadly, I am interested in Riemannian geometry and geometric analysis. More specifically, my background and training is in spectral geometry, where one is interested in the interplay between the geometry of a manifold and the spectrum of some geometrically motivated differential operator (e.g. the Laplace-Beltrami operator). In my PhD thesis I investigated the singularities of the wave trace on lens spaces and flat manifolds (the so-called Poisson relation). After getting my PhD, I spent a year at Université Laval, where I was a CRM Postdoctoral Fellow in the Department of Mathematics. At Laval I worked with Alexandre Girouard on questions related to the Steklov spectrum.

Recently, I have branched out to investigate questions motivated by mathematical physics, fluid mechanics and numerical analysis. Additionally, I am getting increasingly interested in Data Science and Machine Learning. For more information about these projects, see the more detailed discussion below or my GitHub page.

  • Current projects/interests
    • Semi-classical incarnations of the Inverse Spectral Method: The inverse spectral method has been used to study, among other things, the continuous limit of the Toda lattice and the dispersionless limit of the KdV equation. Alejandro Uribe and I are investigating an analog of the inverse spectral method for a class of "tri-diagonal" Toeplitz operators obtained from quantizing the 2-sphere. The work is inspired by this article.
    • Point vortex dynamics: Point vortices provide a useful toy model for helping to understand vorticity in fluid flows. They also have many connections to other areas of mathematics, e.g. dynamical systems, Hamiltonian dynamics and potential theory. Jörn Zimmerling and I are mentoring three undergraduates in a Lab of Geometry project where we investigate the dynamics of the vortices whose motion is confined to a curved surface (like a sphere or a torus). The video to the right is a simulation of three planar vortices in an equilateral triangle configuration where the vortices are known to rotate.
    • Machine Learning and Data Science: Motivated in part by the increasing importance of these disciplines, I've had fun reading up on Machine Learning and Data Science. I'm posting some of my projects on my GitHub page.
